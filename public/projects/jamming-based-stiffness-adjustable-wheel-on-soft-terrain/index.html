Jamming-Based Stiffness-Adjustable Wheel on Soft Terrain

Master's research on a stiffness-adjustable wheel using jamming mechanisms for improved soft-terrain mobility.

Project Overview

Traversing soft terrain is a major challenge for planetary wheeled rovers because wheel-terrain interaction can quickly increase slip and energy consumption. This project proposes and validates a novel jamming-based stiffness-adjustable wheel that modulates wheel stiffness by controlling cable tension routed inside the wheel outer rim.

The wheel architecture consists of a core body, outer rim, inner flexure, and a cable tension mechanism. By tuning tension, the wheel changes structural stiffness and contact behavior on deformable terrain, allowing better adaptability under varying vertical and traction loads.

Research Context

This project is my master’s thesis work, and I am the first author of the related paper: Design and Development of Jamming-Based Stiffness-Adjustable Wheel on Soft Terrain (Journal of Terramechanics, 2025).

The study evaluates longitudinal traversability performance through slip ratio and current consumption under different stiffness levels, vertical loads, and traction loads.

Objectives

  • Identify the key mobility limitations of rigid/high-stiffness wheels on soft terrain.
  • Design a wheel that can continuously modulate stiffness without relying on pre-stress-based architectures.
  • Quantify how stiffness modulation affects slip ratio, travel distance, and current consumption.
  • Validate whether a lower-stiffness configuration improves traversability across payload/traction conditions.

My Contribution

I owned the project end-to-end as thesis lead, from problem definition to publication:

  • Identified the research gap and formulated the stiffness-adjustable wheel concept for soft terrain mobility.
  • Led conceptual design of the jamming-based mechanism and system architecture.
  • Performed mechanical design, simulation, fabrication planning, and prototype development.
  • Built and integrated the test setup for wheel stiffness measurement and soft-terrain traversability evaluation.
  • Executed experiments across load/traction conditions and analyzed slip ratio and electrical current trends.
  • Synthesized results into thesis and first-author journal publication.

Key Results

  • Wheel stiffness range was validated between 17.05 and 22.55 N/mm (average values by test condition).
  • The lower-stiffness configuration corresponds to about 75.6% of the high-stiffness configuration.
  • In the most significant condition (6 kg vertical load, 1.5 kg traction load), the lower-stiffness wheel achieved 155.08% longer traveled distance.
  • Lower stiffness reduced current consumption by up to 67.18% compared with high stiffness.
  • Overall, high stiffness showed higher slip ratio and higher current consumption in most tested soft-terrain conditions.

These findings are consistent with prior wheel-terrain mechanics literature: increasing contact patch via compliant deformation improves traction generation and reduces detrimental slip behavior on soft terrain.

Future Work

  • Extend experiments to additional terrain types and broader payload ranges.
  • Develop automatic/active stiffness adjustment based on terrain and payload conditions.
  • Add online monitoring and control using wheel state metrics such as slip ratio and motor current.
  • Expand analysis to lateral dynamics, where variable stiffness changes contact patch, lateral shear stress, and self-aligning behavior.
